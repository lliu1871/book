Chapter 13: Homotopy

Chapter 13: Homotopy#

“Do the difficult things while they are easy and do the great things while they are small. A journey of a thousand miles must begin with a single step.”

—Lao Tzu

See also

  • Homotopy at Wikipedia

Definition 40

If \(X\) and \(Y\) are spaces then a homotopy of maps from \(X\) to \(Y\) is a map \(F: X \times I \rightarrow Y\), where \(I=[0,1]\).

Definition 41

Two maps \(f_0, f_1: X \rightarrow Y\) are said to be homotopic if there exists a homotopy \(F: X \times I \rightarrow Y\) such that \(F(x, 0)=f_0(x)\) and \(F(x, 1)=f_1(x)\) for all \(x \in X\).

Definition 42

A map \(f:X \to Y\) is said to be a homotopy equivalence with homotopy inverse \(g\) if there is a map \(g: Y \to X\) such that \(g\circ f \sim 1 x\) and \(f\circ g \sim 1 y\). This relationship is denoted by \(X \sim Y\). One also says, in this case, that \(X\) and \(Y\) have the same homotopy type.

Definition 43

A space is said to be contractible if it is homotopy equivalent to the one-point space.
